<p class="people1-h2"></p>
<p>Professor Fossum graduated with MSc in physics from NTH (presently NTNU) in 1978, and PhD in physics from NTH (presently NTNU) in 1983, followed by postdocoral appointments at Universite de Sherbrooke, Quebec, Canada (1984-85), MIT- Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Chemistry and Physics Departments, USA (1985- 89), Risø National Laboratory, Denmark  (1990). Prof. Fossum worked as Researcher and Associate Professor at NTNU, and since 2003 Full Professor at the Department of Physics, NTNU, including several part-time or full-time sabattical stays: ESPCI-ParisTech, France (2004 and 2016-17) partly as invited Joliot Professor, UFPE-Recife, Brazil (2008), Centre for Advanced Studies (CAS) at the Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters (2011-12), and at PUC-Rio de Janeiro (2012-15) as part-time invited scientist by Conselho Nacional de Desenvolvimento Científico e Tecnológico Ciência sem Fronteiras. </p>


<p class="people1-h2"> Research activities: </p>
<p>Earlier interests are in the areas of non-linear acoustics, low-temperature physics,  phase transitions, glassy systems, and liquid crystals.

<p>Now for a number of years, the general activities are in studies of soft and complex condensed matter systems, i.e. in materials which are easily deformable by external stresses, electric or magnetic fields, or sometimes by thermal fluctuations. 
<p>We have since the late 1990s studied clays (i.e. nano-layered silicates) as physical model system representative for many properties of soft and complex matter.Clays are soft because they are built from complex self-assembled structures of anisotropic colloidal nanoparticles. In interlinked projects, we focus on, and study thoroughly, this one physical system, clay, from different points of view, using different experimental techniques: 

<p>We use (synchrotron) X-ray and neutron scattering, rheometry, MR, table-top experiments, etc, thus studying interconnected complex physical phenomena as they manifest themselves in clays and other soft and complex materials. 
<p>The activities involve active collaborations with researchers and labs located at Institute for Energy Technology at Kjeller, Norway. 
<p>International collaborations include: ESPCI-ParisTech, France, Univ. Amsterdam Netherlands, Univ.Rennes1 France, UFPE-Recife Brazil, UnB-Brasilia Brazil, UFABC-Sao Paulo Brazil, PUC-Rio de Janeiro Brazil, 
USP-Sao Paulo Brazil, Gwangju Inst. of Tech./PLS synchrotron lab. at Postech-Pohang in S-Korea, Niels Bohr Institute at Univ. Copenhagen Denmark, MaxIVlab Lund Sweden, Univ. Havana Cuba, Univ. Manchester UK, Chalmers Univ. Sweden, Harvard Univ. USA, and others
